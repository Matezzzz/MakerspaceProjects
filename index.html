# Blood on the Clocktower *(May 2024 -> Present)* Blood on the Clocktower is a social deduction game that I got to know at a GECo event here in Zurich. Since I had a ton of fun playing the few games, I decided to make the game myself, so I could bring it back to Czechia and play it with my friends there. Luckily, the authors of the game are amazing and the designs for all the tokens are readily available on the [game wiki](https://wiki.bloodontheclocktower.com/Main_Page). The main idea of the game is that each player gets a token that defines their role. Each role has a different ability, and defines which team the player belongs to - either they are evil, and their goal is to help kill everyone, or they are good, and they want to figure out the main evil player. All in all, the game consists of a lot of bluffing, social deduction and puzzle solving, in order to either fool the good team or unravel the evil ones. The main component of the game is the role tokens (defining the role and team of each player) and reminder tokens (helping the narrator to remember the game state and lead the game well). I began to make the first version of the game - I designed the tokens in photoshop, created a JSON of all the roles and reminders in the first set (Trouble Brewing), and wrote python scripts for converting JSON to photoshop data, utilities, and creating the final SVG to print. When I had the designs, I headed to the makerspace and lasercut them out of 5mm plywood. The resulting tokens were then handpainted and varnished, and a felt layer was glued to the back. The result was as follows:
After the game caught on massively and I received some feedback, I decided to make the other sets (Bad Moon Rising & Sects and Violets) too. I changed the design to not include the role description, as that could be found elsewhere, it made the painting much easier and it allowed me to make the role icon even more prominent. I added the JSONs for the next two sets, lasercut the modified design for both the role and reminder tokens and got a better felt material for the back. The final tokens look like this:
There is one more set available - the experimental characters, which can be mixed with the ones from other scripts to create ever more unique games. # Ring of lights *(October 2023 -> March 2024)* I have a ring of lights on the ceiling in my room! These were used by the previous owner of the now turned student house as backlight for psychological sessions held in what is my apartment today. However, the lights were disabled by the previous owner - so with the permission from the current landlord, I decided to try to fix them.
By connecting each light to the power grid individually, I figured the problem is in the controlling network, not the lights themselves, so I decided to fix and improve that. My goal was to be able to dim each light in real time by sending commands either from my pc or any other device. After getting some help from Lukas Pfitscher from the ETH Makerspace about which circuits I would want to use to achieve my goals, I came up with the following design - one arduino will control 5 lights using PWM, it will be able to receive commands via bluetooth. I will use a step down converter to obtain power for the arduino, and RobotDyn AC dimmers will be used to modulate the AC current using the PWM sent by the arduino. Then I will have one control network in the wall, that will be able to signal all the arduinos on the ceiling. I started by creating a demo circuit that is able to control one light by receiving bluetooth commands from my phone:
I have tried designing a container to fit the components (connected by dupont wires) into the lamp, however, this has failed completely, as there was not enough space to fit all the wires and I was unable to close the lamp. For this reason, I have decided to instead design a PCB to hold all the components. Having no experience with PCB design, I first watched a (tutorial series)[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=szu8dJoyikA] (really well done, can recommend) on how to use the KiCAD software. Then I designed the PCB to hold the components I had, and ordered it from JLCPCB. The result was as follows:
I did make some design mistakes - I swapped two of the output pins for the step-down converter and made some of the holes too little for the 230V input - but I was able to fix all of these after the PCBs arrived, and after soldering all the components, I was able to dim the light again. However, despite my initial estimates that the PCB would tightly fit into the lamp, the 230V wires surrounding it made fitting difficult, and since I likely had some contact with the metal casing of the lamp, the circuit failed to work at all. After this failure I had to focus on ETH schoolwork again and I was unable to complete the project. # Face chocolate *(October 2023)* I decided to create a chocolate in the shape of a friends face as a gift for his birthday. I started by taking his photo but converting it directly didn't look so promising, so I looked into edge detection algorithms and ways to convert images to pencil drawings. In the end, I settled on using photoshop to convert the photo to smoothed edges, and used a python script to interpret is as a heightmap. This gave me the basic shape of the 3D model. The first idea I had was to take an inverse of the face, add a joke nickname from a summer camp where we used to go together and to 3D print the whole thing. The result looked quite neat (though with a few 3D print hairs), but sadly, the chocolate was rather subpar. It had a tendency to stick to the form, and to take tiny pieces of plastic with it, making it inedible. Also, the text was mirrored:
Because of this, I looked into alternative ways to construct the mold, and quickly enough, I found a machine that would be able to do just that - the vacuum former at the makerspace can place a plastic sheet over my 3D print, and suck out all the air, forming a food-grade mould. So this is what I went with - after few struggling attempts with the 3D printers (the nozzle kept getting stuck because I was printing at the lowest resolution), I managed to get a succesful print, and created a mold using the vacuum former. The resulting treat looked and tasted great, particularly when cast out of the finest belgian chocolate :D
